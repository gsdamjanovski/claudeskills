## Major Parties

### Australian Labor Party (ALP)

- Centre-left; Australia's oldest party (founded 1891)
- Base: trade unions, progressive middle class, urban seats
- Factional system: Left, Right (historically dominant), some state-specific factions
- Policy traditions: social democracy, workers' rights, public services, multiculturalism

### Liberal Party of Australia

- Centre-right; founded 1944 by Robert Menzies
- Base: business, suburban middle class, professionals, rural towns
- Factional divisions: moderates (small-l liberals) vs conservatives
- Policy traditions: free enterprise, individual liberty, lower taxes, national security

### The Nationals (formerly Country Party/National Party)

- Agrarian conservative; represent rural and regional Australia
- Permanent Coalition partner with Liberals at federal level
- Base: farmers, regional communities, resource industries
- Policy priorities: regional infrastructure, agriculture support, water management

### The Coalition

- Liberal-National alliance governing as single bloc
- Coalition agreement determines ministry allocations
- Nationals typically hold agriculture, infrastructure, regional portfolios
- Occasional tensions between parties on climate, energy, regional policy

## Minor Parties and Crossbench

### Australian Greens

- Left-progressive; environmental focus but broad policy platform
- Strongest in inner-city seats (Melbourne, Brisbane, Sydney)
- Consistent Senate presence; occasionally holds balance of power
- Policy priorities: climate action, social justice, democratic reform, housing

### Teal Independents

- Emerged 2022 election; won traditionally safe Liberal seats
- Profile: moderate on economics, progressive on climate and integrity
- Concentrated in wealthy urban/suburban electorates
- Climate 200 backing; community-based campaign model
- Key figures change election to election — always verify current membership

### Other Senate Crossbenchers

- Composition varies significantly each parliament
- May include: Jacqui Lambie Network, One Nation, United Australia Party, David Pocock, others
- Always search for current Senate composition
- Individual crossbenchers often have specific issue priorities

### Historical Minor Parties

- Australian Democrats (1977-2008): centrist; "keep the bastards honest"
- Democratic Labor Party: anti-communist Labor splinter; influential 1950s-70s
- One Nation: populist right; intermittent Senate presence since 1997

## Electoral System

### House of Representatives

- **Full preferential voting** (instant-runoff)
- 151 single-member electorates
- Voter must number every box
- Preferences distributed until one candidate has absolute majority
- Redistributions occur based on population shifts

### Senate

- **Single transferable vote** with group voting tickets abolished (2016)
- 6 senators per state elected each half-Senate election; 12 at double dissolution
- 2 senators per territory each election
- Quota: approximately 14.3% for half-Senate election
- Above-the-line: number at least 6 party boxes
- Below-the-line: number at least 12 individual candidates

### Election Timing

- House term: maximum 3 years from first sitting
- Half-Senate elections: coincide with House elections
- PM advises Governor-General on election date
- Double dissolution: requires trigger (twice-rejected legislation)
- Writs issued minimum 33 days before polling day

### Compulsory Voting

- Mandatory enrolment and voting for citizens 18+
- Small fine for non-compliance
- Produces high turnout (~90%+); shapes campaign strategy
- Centrist median voter more important than base mobilisation

## Electoral History and Patterns

### Government Alternation

- Labor and Coalition have alternated government throughout post-war era
- Typical pattern: 2-4 term governments
- Three-term governments reasonably common; four terms rare

### Swing Seats and Safe Seats

- Marginal seats (<6% margin) concentrated in outer suburbs, regional centres
- Inner-city seats trending Labor/Greens
- Wealthy urban seats: Liberal heartland under pressure from teals
- Regional Queensland and WA: historically swinging; trending conservative

### Recent Electoral Shifts

- Decline of major party primary vote (both Labor and Coalition)
- Rise of independents and minor parties
- Climate as wedge issue in traditionally Liberal seats
- Housing affordability emerging as electoral driver
- Always search for analysis of most recent election — patterns evolve

### Key Demographic Factors

- Age: younger voters lean Labor/Greens; older voters lean Coalition
- Education: university graduates increasingly progressive
- Home ownership: renters lean left; owners lean right
- Urban/regional divide: cities progressive; regions conservative (with exceptions)
